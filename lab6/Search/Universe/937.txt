The book warned that comic books were a negative form of popular literature and a serious cause of juvenile delinquency. A minor bestseller, it created alarm in parents and galvanized them to campaign for censorship. Publishers All American Comics, Comic Media, Fiction House, Star Publications, and Youthful go out of business. February[edit] Hopalong Cassidy, with issue #86, revived by DC Comics, taking over the numbering of the Fawcett Comics series.[1] March[edit] Atlas Comics publishes Lorna, the Jungle Girl #6 (renamed from Lorna, the Jungle Queen) April[edit] April 21–22: United States Senate Subcommittee on Juvenile Delinquency hearings on the comic book industry. June[edit] "Tralla La," a Scrooge McDuck story by Carl Barks, published in Uncle Scrooge #6 June 4: Further meeting of United States Senate Subcommittee on Juvenile Delinquency September[edit] Formation of the Comics Magazine Association of America (CMAA) and the Comics Code Authority (CCA) Atlas Comics publishes Crime Fighters Always Win #11 (renamed from Timely's Crimefighters) Roy of the Rovers debuts in Fleetway's Tiger November[edit] The Haunt of Fear, with issue #28 (November/December cover date), canceled by EC Comics. December[edit] Kodansha publishes Nakayoshi. The Vault of Horror, with issue #40 (December 1954/January 1955 cover date), canceled by EC Comics. Chamber of Chills, with issue #26, canceled by Harvey Comics.
